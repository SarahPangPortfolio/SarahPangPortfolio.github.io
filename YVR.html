Overview

Nov. 2022 - Dec. 2022 (5 Weeks)

YVR's Innovative Travel Solutions ( ITS) team is looking to expand the reach of indigenous artwork residing within the airport, specifically to individuals that are unable to visit the physical locations. This is part of the airport's reconciliation efforts with not only the Musqueam, their land being occupied by YVR, but also all the indigenous communities in British Columbia. Our team is given the opportunity to investigate the different possible mediums used to virtually house the indigenous artwork in YVR. Discovering examples of already existing digital museums that provide an immersive experience that guides the viewer through the space.

Investigating Existing Digital Museums

As the leader and point of contact of the group, I suggested investigating different types of remote digital museums and analyzing the unique interfaces of each. The thought process was to have users evaluate the interface of each and therefore suggest a starting point in the ITS team can use. By physically visiting the YVR airport and going on a tour of the artwork, I was able to have a deeper understanding of what the user flow would look like. With this knowledge, my group and I researched different digital museums which I further analyzed and categorized into interactive web gallery, interactive scrolling timeline, 360 virtual museum tour, and 3D space capture.

Study

Using the "how might we" statement and the insights gathered from the interview and user research, our group conducted 10 minutes of brainstorming where each person wrote down as many solutions as they could on sticky notes and drawings. With the ideas, the team would analyze the suggestions and plot the solutions on a graph between desireability and interactivity. The process was conducted twice before taking the the most desired and interactive sitkcy notes were categorized and developed further. The final proposed solution is a combination of my solution of interactive gallery of stories and a groupmate's idea of customization of breast icons.

Solution

Our proposed design includes an extension of Hand's website whis has an interactive space where customers can connect with one another and share personal stories and experiences. The solution uses breast icons to represent individuals, and displays written stories when the icons are selected. In order to maintain the brand identity, our team aimed to maintain a fun and light-hearted approach, inspired by Hands' designs, despite the serious problem space. We adopted Tough Titties Designs' simple visual design and branding colors, and added customizable boob shapes to enhance their existing variety. With a mockup, my group and I conducted user testing using questionnaires, interviews, and think-aloud method. The feedback provided major insights on the mockup's pain points which were further iterated on in the solution.

Idea Categorization

User Testing

The Card

Key Points:

  1. Emphasis on calling for action
  2. Clearly stated purpose and goal

In order to bridge the physical and real-life customers onto one digital platform, the card will be sent or given to the customer after each purchase. Through user testing, we found that it was important to have a clear purpose of card, so we went through many iterations to ensure strong call to action with a direct layout. Being careful to not overload the card with information and leave some mystery and ambiguity to lead the customer to explore more.

Onboarding

Key Points:

  1. Telling the users what to expect
  2. Introduce the purpose of the site

Once the customer reaches the site either from Hand's main website or through the card, the user is greeted with an onboarding page. This page would not only serve as instructions on how to use the site, but also an introduction as to what the purpose is about. Providing an explorative context to the website and prepare the users for what to be expected. Through user testing, we found that users were unsure what their actions would do at the gallery, thus hints were included to provide reasonings behind the specific features.

Gallery

Key Points:

  1. Display of 10 random titties
  2. Filtering through search bar and filter button
  3. Shuffle the 10 titties display

After the onboarding page, the user would be brought to the gallery where 10 random pairs of titties would be displayed. This is meant to mimic the real life gathering experience, where one would never know who they will meet. By scrolling to the very end, a shuffle button is present to let users explore a new set of stories as the 10 pairs of titties are changed into new ones. A filtering and search feature was also added to help users quickly find the stories in which they resonate with. After reciving feedback from both Hands and users, a shop button was added to link the gallery with the products that Hands offers. The interaction on this page is hovering where the titty would not only enlarge and change color, but also display the name associated with the story to make the interaction more rewarding.

Create Your Story

Key Points:

  1. The progress bar at the top
  2. Code checking status
  3. Help link for user assistance

To create one's own story, the user can select "Your Story +" which would be directed to an access code page. Here the user can input the code from the physical card which is also hinted at through the help link. This is to prevent users, who have not purchased items from Hands before, from creating a story as they would not be considered part of Hands' customer base. The progressbar at the top also addresses the heuristic of visibility of system status which helps the user know at what point of the process they are at as well as what is to be expected next.

Customization

Key Points:

  1. “I don't feel represented” button
  2. Size adjustment and controls
  3. Ability to choose nothing for the customization
  4. Respects on privacies
  5. Emphasis on breast cancer survivors

Following the access code, the user will be brought to the customization page where user can express themselves through Hands' drawings and also contribute to the gallery with their own identity. The design allows for a large combination of customizations to make the users feel represented as much as possible as well as an "I don't feel represented" button to further elaborate on what is missing through feedback. In the case that the user does not want to create an icon, the default image would be a smiley face to promote privacy. Similarly with the display name, users can leave the name blank to have the story be displayed as "Anonymous". The only required fields are the message itself as well as categorizing the message being related to breast cancer or not. This was added after the user testing when the issue of wanting to view stories that promoted over all women empowerment rather than breast cancer was brought up. Lastly, the confirmation states what is to happen next in order for the uwer's story to be part of the database and the user can return back to the gallery.

View Story

Key Points:

  1. Stamping to allow engagement
  2. Features improving the intimacy of the experience

When the viewer selects a pair of titties, the viewer would be brough to the story in which the user has created. Here, a stamping function is available for the viewer to either appreciate, support or admire the story through selecting the approriate sticker and placing it on the page. Features that improve the intimacy of the experience include the social media link, sticker ratio bar, and the number in which other people are viewing the story. These functions are to help create a community where viewers and users do not feel alone as well as be seen, creating a positive enviornment and oppertunities to reach out.
